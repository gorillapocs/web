Hantavirus Prevention Guide

Essential information to protect yourself and your community

What is Hantavirus?

Hantaviruses are a group of viruses carried by rodents. Humans can become infected through contact with rodent saliva, urine, or droppings, or by inhaling aerosolized particles from contaminated environments. There is no person-to-person transmission for most hantavirus strains (except Andes virus in South America, which has rare person-to-person spread).

Two Main Diseases

HPS (Americas)

Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome

  • Fever, fatigue, muscle aches
  • Headaches, dizziness, chills
  • Cough, shortness of breath
  • Fluid buildup in lungs
  • Case fatality rate: ~35-40%

HFRS (Eurasia)

Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome

  • Fever, chills, headache
  • Back/abdominal pain
  • Blurred vision, red eyes
  • Kidney failure
  • Case fatality rate: 1-15%

Prevention Measures

  • Seal up gaps and holes in homes to prevent rodent entry
  • Trap rodents in and around your home using snap traps
  • Keep food in rodent-proof containers and clean up food waste
  • When cleaning rodent-contaminated areas, wear gloves and an N95 mask
  • Do NOT sweep or vacuum rodent droppings — this aerosolizes the virus
  • Disinfect contaminated surfaces with a 10% bleach solution, wait 5 minutes, then wipe up
  • Ventilate closed buildings for at least 30 minutes before entering
  • Avoid sleeping on the ground in rodent-endemic areas; use elevated cots
  • Keep campsites clean and store food in rodent-proof containers
  • In endemic areas, consider rodent control programs around dwellings

If You Think You've Been Exposed

  • Seek medical attention immediately — early treatment is critical
  • Tell your doctor about potential rodent exposure
  • Monitor for symptoms for up to 8 weeks after exposure
  • There is no specific cure — treatment is supportive (ICU care, oxygen therapy)
  • Early admission to ICU significantly improves survival chances
